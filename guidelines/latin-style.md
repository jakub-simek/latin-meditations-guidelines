# Ecclesiastical Latin Style

## Language

- The meditation itself is written in Ecclesiastical Latin.
- Direct quotations may remain in the original language when required by the
  citation rules, but the surrounding meditation remains Latin.
- The style should be elevated, calm, contemplative, and rhythmic.
- Prefer biblical, liturgical, patristic, scholastic, and traditional ascetical
  diction.
- Allow measured rhetorical amplification where it deepens devotion without
  obscuring clarity.
- Address the soul directly and rhetorically when appropriate, normally as
  self-address such as `anima mea`.
- Avoid unmarked shifts between `anima mea`, `anima tua`, `nos`, and direct
  address to an external reader.

## Orthography

- Use no accent marks in the running Latin meditation text.
- Use a consistent i/j and u/v pattern within a project.
- Use `alleluia` as the standard spelling in normalized Latin text.
- Preserve another spelling only when deliberately reproducing a source
  diplomatically.
- Use plain `ae` and `oe` as the normal working orthography.
- Ligatures may be used in a final typeset version only if applied consistently.

## Avoid

- modern colloquialisms
- neologisms unless unavoidable
- overly abstract academic terminology
- sentimental or psychological phrasing foreign to traditional ascetical
  language
- inflationary use of `aperire` for general meanings such as develop, explain,
  unfold, or introduce; use it only when the narrower sense to open is intended

## Final Latin Pass

Before finishing, check especially:

- agreement of subject, verb, adjective, pronoun, and apposition
- case government and prepositional usage
- tense, mood, voice, and imperative forms
- consistency of singular and plural address
- idiomatic ecclesiastical Latin rather than calques from modern languages
