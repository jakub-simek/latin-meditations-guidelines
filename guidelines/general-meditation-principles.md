# General Principles for Latin Meditations

These rules are the shared baseline for traditional Catholic Latin meditation
projects. Individual projects may add stricter or more specific rules in a
local overlay.

## Orientation

- Each meditation is theological, contemplative, and ascetical, not merely
  explanatory or academic.
- Each meditation begins from a concrete primary text: for example a liturgical
  chant or lesson, a patristic or medieval sermon, a chapter of St. Alphonsus,
  or another approved spiritual source.
- The purpose is the interior assimilation of that primary text: not only
  exegesis, but spiritual interpretation and appropriation.
- The theology is rooted in traditional Catholic doctrine and avoids modernist,
  psychologizing, or merely subjective tendencies.
- The normal form is a classical discursive meditation, akin to traditional
  lectio, consideratio, or spiritual homily, rather than a strictly Ignatian
  exercise.
- Ignatian elements such as compositio loci, application of the senses, ordered
  points, colloquies, election, or examen may be used only when they organically
  serve the primary text; they are not the governing structure by default.

## Center of the Meditation

- Keep the primary text visibly and materially at the center.
- Other sources illuminate the primary text; they should not replace it or turn
  the meditation into a parallel essay.
- If related meditations already exist in the project, read them first and avoid
  needless repetition while allowing deliberate thematic harmony.

## Normal Movement

Each meditation normally includes:

- a title
- a Textus fundamentalis section quoting the primary text or the relevant part
  of it
- a recognizable spiritual exposition of the text
- biblical, theological, patristic, medieval, magisterial, or ascetical
  amplification as appropriate to the project
- spiritual application
- a brief conclusion returning to the central theme

The usual headings may be used, adapted, or omitted when the movement is still
clear:

- `## Textus fundamentalis`
- `## Expositio spiritualis`
- `## Amplificatio biblica`
- `## Resonantiae patristicae et mediaevales`
- `## Applicatio spiritualis`
- `## Conclusio`

## Marian Dimension

- A Marian component should normally be included according to the principle
  `de Maria numquam satis`.
- It must arise organically from the primary text, its theology, or the
  meditation's spiritual movement.
- Mary is especially contemplated as exemplar of faith, image of the Church,
  Mother of the faithful, and model of interior assimilation.
- The Marian component must not feel like an appended devotion.

## Length

- Normal length: about 700-1000 words.
- Shorter meditations may be about 500-700 words when the primary text is narrow
  or the form is intentionally concise.
- Longer meditations may reach about 1200 words when source integration or
  theological development requires it.
- The length serves contemplative development, not completeness for its own
  sake.
