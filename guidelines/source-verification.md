# Source Verification

## General Rule

- Never invent quotations or reconstruct them from memory.
- Direct quotations require a reliable source and substantial fidelity to that
  source.
- Minor orthographic, typographical, or edition-based variants are acceptable
  when the wording remains identifiable and faithful.
- For paraphrases, cite with `cf.` and avoid making the reference look like a
  checked direct quotation.

## Scripture and Traditional Sources

- Use the biblical numbering and textual tradition specified by the local
  project.
- Give precise references for patristic and medieval authors: author, work, and
  internal division where possible.
- PL or PG references may be added as supplementary references, but should not
  replace work and section references.

## Thomas Aquinas

- Use standard scholastic reference forms, e.g. `Summa Theologiae` II-II,
  q. 23, a. 1, ad 2.
- Quotations from Thomas should preferably be checked against Corpus
  Thomisticum.
- Thomas may be used as a precise witness to patristic authorities he explicitly
  transmits, but do not use the `Summa` merely as a convenient quarry for
  patristic quotations.

## Catechismus Romanus

- When the Roman Catechism is used, cite by Pars and caput, adding a more
  precise division when available.
- A local project may specify the normal working edition.
- Direct quotations should still be checked against an image or reliable printed
  edition when feasible.

## St. Alphonsus Maria de Liguori

- Cite by work and chapter, section, or number.
- Quotations should preferably be checked against a reliable edition or the
  IntraText Digital Library when available.
- Direct quotations must preserve the original language and original
  orthography.
- Italian direct quotations remain in Italian and preserve spelling, accents,
  punctuation, capitalization, and typographic apostrophes.
- Typographic apostrophes may be corrected when a digital source uses straight
  or otherwise wrong apostrophe characters.
